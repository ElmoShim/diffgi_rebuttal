\noindent We thank the reviewers for their feedback. In the camera-ready we will fix the GIMDiffusion citation ([15]$\to$[11]), adopt ``multi-chart geometry image'' consistently, and clarify that TSDF (l.\,139--141) is a 2D pixel-to-contour signed distance in UV space.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{\rev{rPcg} Key Contribution: End-to-End Differentiability via DMS.} Prior GI works use non-differentiable mesh extraction, preventing 3D-domain supervision from reaching the latent space. DMS closes this gap, enabling the Normal Rendering Loss to be computed in 3D and back-propagated. As shown in Tab. 2, adding this Normal Loss (enabled solely by DMS) reduces JSD by $43\%$ and lifts NC from $0.92$ to $0.96$.

\noindent\textbf{\rev{hEpV} Mesh Extraction \& Fair Evaluation.} To correct a descriptive error in Supp.~Sec.~5: as stated in Sec.~4.2 (l.~275--277), DMS is applied \emph{only} to TSDF, while quad tessellation—the method used in Omages—is used for occupancy-based baselines. As shown in Tab.~\ref{tab:rebuttal_results}, while GarmageNet's official reconstruction pipeline improves boundary quality, this heavily engineered process drastically increases computational time (${\sim}360\times$) and introduces occasional failures. Consequently, unifying the extraction with simple tessellation is considered a fairer evaluation of the models' raw generative capabilities.

\noindent\textbf{\rev{rPcg} Efficiency vs. Quality Trade-off.} GarmageNet builds a more compact latent space, achieving faster diffusion generation. However, its raw representation is ${\sim}30\times$ larger than our multi-chart GI. This forces extreme compression (Tab.~\ref{tab:comp}), leading to condition mismatches and geometric detail loss (Tab.~1, 5, and Fig.~6 of main paper). Moreover, its complete pipeline relies on a heavy reconstruction step (${\sim}5.1$\,s, Tab.~\ref{tab:rebuttal_results}), pushing the total latency to ${\sim}8$\,s. In contrast, our approach directly generates high-quality geometry with accurate conditional shapes and fine wrinkles. By achieving this with true \emph{end-to-end} sub-second efficiency, our method optimally balances the efficiency-quality trade-off.

\noindent\textbf{\rev{hEpV} Choice of VAE Architecture.} GarmageNet's panel-wise VAE aggressively compresses panels into 64-D vectors, making it incompatible with standard image VAEs. In contrast, our multi-chart GI is a standard 2D image, naturally admitting the SD 1.5 VAE to benefit from pretrained weights (similar to GIMDiffusion). This is purely for convergence acceleration. Fig.~\ref{fig:vae} confirms a from-scratch VAE reaches comparable capacity, albeit slower and with instability. Furthermore, Tab. 2 of main paper isolates our representation's effect: with fixed VAE initialization, switching from Occ to TSDF independently and significantly improves reconstruction.

\noindent\textbf{\rev{rPcg} Comparison with GIMDiffusion.} GIMDiffusion remains unreleased (code, weights, data), preventing direct reproduction. However, its geometry pipeline (binary-mask GI + SD-initialized VAE + LDM) is structurally equivalent to our "Occ w/o $\mathcal{L}_\text{Normal}$" baseline. Against this configuration, our full method (TSDF + $\mathcal{L}_\text{Normal}$) improves CD by 3.3$\times$ and JSD by 3.6$\times$ (Tab. 2).

\noindent\textbf{\rev{ZMhC, hEpV} Characteristics of multi-chart-GI-based diffusion models.} Our method inherits properties of this family: (i) Seams: A shared structural limitation; yet, we substantially reduce boundary errors (BCD, Tab. 5). Exploring alternative representations (e.g., dual contouring) is future work. (ii) UV dependency: Training on industry-standard UVs yields meshes with clean UVs, directly usable in simulation pipelines. (iii) Resolution: As an image-based approach, it scales freely; we adopt 256$\times$256 based on our convergence analysis (Supp. Sec. 1).

\noindent\textbf{\rev{ZMhC} Preprocessing Time.} Conversion takes 0.2--0.4\,s per mesh; details will be added in the camera-ready.
