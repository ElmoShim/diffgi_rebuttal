\noindent We thank the reviewers for their feedback. In the camera-ready we will fix the GIMDiffusion citation ([15]$\to$[11]) and adopt ``multi-chart geometry image'' consistently. [R3 minor]

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{[R3] DMS-based end-to-end differentiability \emph{to mesh} is the key contribution.} Prior GI works (Omages, GIMDiffusion, GarmageNet) are differentiable only up to image-domain pixel losses; mesh extraction is a non-differentiable post-process, so 3D-domain supervision cannot reach the latent. DMS closes this gap, enabling Normal Rendering Loss to be computed in 3D and back-propagated to the VAE encoder/decoder (Supp.~Sec.~6: ``$\mathcal{L}_\text{Normal}$ can propagate gradients \dots\ \emph{only because the DMS module is fully differentiable}''). Tab.~2 isolates the two effects. Representation alone (Occ$\rightarrow$TSDF) gives CD $1.503\!\rightarrow\!0.595\!\times\!10^{-3}$ ($-60\%$). Adding the Normal Loss---usable \emph{only} via DMS---further improves NC $0.921\!\rightarrow\!0.961$ and JSD $2.169\!\rightarrow\!1.244\!\times\!10^{-3}$. TSDF~+~DMS~+~$\mathcal{L}_\text{Normal}$ is therefore a coupled contribution that enables 3D end-to-end learning, not a sum of standard components.

\noindent\textbf{[R1] Isolating the effect of SD~1.5 VAE initialization.} We use the SD~1.5 VAE only to accelerate convergence; trained on in-the-wild RGB, it carries no geometry-specific prior. We address R1's concern---``the gain may be confounded between representation and a stronger VAE''---in two steps. \textbf{(i)} Tab.~2 \emph{fixes} the VAE init and varies only the representation: Occ$\rightarrow$TSDF reduces CD $1.503\!\rightarrow\!0.595\!\times\!10^{-3}$ ($-60\%$), demonstrating that the representation contributes independently. \textbf{(ii)} We are further running a from-scratch-vs.-SD-pretrained VAE comparison showing the two converge to comparable final performance (the latter only faster); this will be included in the supplementary. % TODO: from-scratch vs SD-pretrained VAE convergence/final-performance experiment

\noindent\textbf{[R1] Rationale for the unified DMS evaluation protocol.} We agree that mesh extraction can affect outcomes. For this reason we apply the same extractor (DMS) to every baseline; on binary occupancy DMS produces a result equivalent to standard Marching Squares (Supp.~Sec.~3), so no baseline is structurally disadvantaged. The protocol (Supp.~Sec.~5) is designed to isolate \emph{representation and compression} from mesh-extraction engineering---each baseline's native pipeline embeds different boundary heuristics that would otherwise mask the representation's own contribution. Under this protocol our method retains its advantage on both ABO and GarmageSet (Tab.~1). We are also preparing a mini comparison verifying the visual and numerical equivalence of DMS and standard MS on occupancy inputs, to be included in the supplementary. % TODO: DMS = standard MS equivalence (occupancy input) mini experiment / visualization

\noindent\textbf{[R3] Why no direct GIMDiffusion comparison.} GIMDiffusion's code, weights, and preprocessed dataset are all unreleased, preventing same-condition reproduction. Nevertheless, its geometry pipeline (binary-mask GI + SD-initialized VAE + LDM; Collaborative Control is texture-only) is structurally equivalent to our \emph{``Occ w/o $\mathcal{L}_\text{Normal}$''} ablation row. Against this baseline, our method (TSDF~+~$\mathcal{L}_\text{Normal}$) improves CD $1.503\!\rightarrow\!0.461\!\times\!10^{-3}$ ($3.3\times$) and JSD $4.539\!\rightarrow\!1.244\!\times\!10^{-3}$ ($3.6\times$, Tab.~2). We will make this relationship explicit in the camera-ready.

\noindent\textbf{[R3] Speed vs.\ quality trade-off and structural limits of GarmageNet.} GarmageNet's extreme 72-dim-per-panel compression yields fast diffusion but explicit quality cost: image-to-3D BCD $5.64$ vs.\ ours $2.91\!\times\!10^{-2}$ ($\sim\!2\times$ worse, Tab.~5); reconstruction CD on GarmageSet $2.19$ vs.\ $0.46\!\times\!10^{-3}$ ($4.7\times$ worse, Tab.~1). Fig.~6 (main paper) further shows GarmageNet violating the input condition's structural attributes across multiple rows---garment-type switches (row~1: skirt$\rightarrow$t-shirt; row~4: shorts$\rightarrow$skirt), sleeve presence/length mismatches, and form breakdown with anomalous cuff in row~5. Its fixed per-panel structure also \emph{precludes} application to variable-panel domains such as ABO (l.\,274--275). Our method retains $\sim\!1$\,s single-GPU latency while improving both quality and generality.

\noindent\textbf{[R2] On UV-parameterization dependency.} UV reliance is a constraint shared across the multi-chart GI family, ours included. In our target domains---garments and furniture---industry-standard UVs are typically available (ABO 7{,}900 commercial furniture; GarmageSet 14{,}801 professionally designed garments). As a side benefit, training on well-parameterized data yields meshes whose UVs are also clean, making them directly usable in simulation and texturing pipelines.

\noindent\textbf{[R2] Resolution and latent compression.} Supp.~Sec.~1 shows reconstruction error converges beyond resolution~256---and even with $8\times$ spatial compression to a $32\!\times\!32\!\times\!4$ latent we outperform the uncompressed Omages ($64\!\times\!64\!\times\!4$) on CD/JSD/NC, because TSDF's redundant boundary encoding is robust to latent compression. Scaling to higher resolution simply enlarges the latent grid.

\noindent\textbf{[R1] Seams in Fig.~6.} Seams are a structural limitation of the multi-chart GI family, shared by all baselines. Our method substantially reduces boundary error in BCD (Tab.~5); complete removal is left as future work via dual-contouring integration (Sec.~5, Limitations).

\noindent\textbf{[R1] TSDF is a 2D distance in UV-image space, not 3D (l.\,139--141).} For each pixel, the signed Euclidean distance to the nearest patch contour (positive inside a chart, negative outside), truncated at 15 pixels on the $1024\!\times\!1024$ grid (Sec.~3.1, Step~4). It is not a 3D point-to-surface distance. We will state this more clearly in the main text.

\noindent\textbf{[R3] Non-cherry-picked ABO generation.} ABO results are already in Tabs.~1, 4 and Fig.~5. To address cherry-picking concerns, Fig.~\ref{fig:abo} provides a latent-interpolation sequence between two unrelated samples on ABO---globally consistent, plausible furniture geometries appear throughout the entire path.
